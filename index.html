About the Dataset

One controversial topic that has been the subject of debate for decades is the existence and extent of the gender pay gap. While some argue that it is a thing of the past or is overstated, others maintain that it is a persistent and systemic problem that contributes to gender inequality in the workforce. The topic is complex and multifaceted, involving factors such as occupational segregation, discrimination, and societal expectations. Despite ongoing efforts to address the issue, there is still a significant gap between male and female earnings in many industries and professions, leading to ongoing controversy and debate in the United States. This dataset from the Bureau of Labor Statistics provides a comprehensive look at the median weekly incomes of 535 different occupations broken down by gender. This data can be used to analyze the existence and magnitude of the gender pay gap in different industries. The dataset can be found here

The Arguments in the visualizations
The left chart is persuasive because it uses a clear and straightforward presentation of data that is easy to understand at a glance. The use of color coding to distinguish between male and female workers is effective in emphasizing the higher number of female workers in most of the categories, which challenges the notion that men dominate certain industries.

The rhetorical framing techniques used in the left chart are:
  • Omission: The graph only shows the categories of occupation where the female workforce is larger. Other categories and actual wages of females in these occupations are omitted in the visualization to prove that gender gap does not exist. In fact, females are employed more than men.
  • Comparison: The left chart uses grouped bars to compare the statistics of the number of female and male workers to emphasize its argument.
  • Anchoring: The left viz directs the viewer's attention to only focus on the comparison and interpret the data by looking at the contrasting information being presented
The right chart is persuasive because it uses a clear and straightforward presentation of data that is easy to understand at a glance. The use of color coding to distinguish between male and female weekly wage is effectively emphasizing in females earning significantly less than their male counterparts in those job categories making it difficult to deny the existence of the gender pay gap.

The rhetorical framing techniques used in the right chart are:
  • Omission: This graph also only shows the categories of occupation where the female weekly wage is lesser than the male weekly pay. Other categories in these occupations are omitted in the visualization to prove that gender gap does exist.
  • Comparison: The right chart uses multiple lines to draw a comparison between the wage of female and male workers to emphasize its argument.
  • Anchoring: The left viz directs the viewer's attention to only focus on the comparison and interpret the data by looking at the contrasting information being presented.
  • Axis Scaling: The right visualization scales the axis to make the pay gap seem larger than it actually may be.
